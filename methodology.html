Statutory standard

A thorough, accurate, and reliable documentary record.

Under 22 U.S.C. § 4351(a), the FRUS series documents major U.S. foreign policy decisions and significant diplomatic activity. This portal keeps FRUS documentary evidence distinct from statistical context, legal authority, and archival discovery.

Why 1861–1992?

The project begins in 1861 to align the historical experience with the surviving annual and later topical FRUS series represented in the repository’s discovery index and to include Civil War-era resource diplomacy as the earliest research horizon. It ends in 1992 as a deliberate Cold War-era boundary and as the terminal year of the checked-in FRUS subject corpus.

These are project boundaries, not claims that strategic-resource diplomacy began or ended in either year. Post-1992 relevance appears only in a visually separate Modern Context layer.

Why FRUS is the narrative spine

Foreign Relations of the United States is the official documentary historical record of major U.S. foreign-policy decisions and significant diplomatic activity. It provides an edited route into how officials described problems, evaluated options, and recorded decisions.

Important limit

FRUS is selected and edited. It is not the complete archive. An absence from FRUS does not establish an absence of U.S. interest, deliberation, or action.

The full index uses subject-authority assignments and table-of-contents context from the repository’s FRUS Subjects data. A subject assignment is a discovery signal. Volume dates and chapter headings are navigation context; they are not silently presented as document-level dates, titles, or summaries.

Only records with explicit document-level pilot metadata receive the “Reviewed FRUS document” label. Every other record remains a “FRUS discovery lead.”

Official-source hierarchy

  1. Foreign Relations of the United States
  2. National Archives and Records Administration
  3. U.S. Geological Survey and historical Bureau of Mines publications
  4. Department of State treaty publications and Statutes at Large
  5. U.S. Census Bureau and Department of Commerce historical statistics
  6. GSA, National Defense Stockpile, Defense, presidential-library, Federal Register, congressional, Library of Congress, CIA, and GAO records

Non-governmental sources may assist discovery or clarification but are not presented as equivalent to official primary sources. Each source registry entry states its agency, scope, tier, URL, and use note.

Country-year intelligence briefs

A country page combines two distinct views: the complete set of records currently linked to the historical entity and a selected-year briefing assembled from curated country-period records. FRUS remains the first layer. The country brief follows it with political facts, resource context, instruments, NARA discovery plans, and explicit research gaps.

  • The selected year is stored in the page URL and is always limited to 1861–1992.
  • A fact marked “Verified” carries a link to a source-registry record or reviewed FRUS document.
  • A fact marked “Unknown” remains blank; the interface does not estimate or interpolate it.
  • Country production, exports, U.S. supplier share, and world rank require country-specific official series.
  • USGS observations for the United States or world may appear as context. They are never relabeled as bilateral or country statistics.
  • If the selected year has no normalized benchmark, the nearest source year within five years may be shown with its actual year clearly displayed.
  • A NARA query plan is an archival discovery aid, not a verified catalog result or substantive finding.
Interpretive boundary

“Why this country mattered” appears only when a curated period record names its supporting FRUS and official sources. Outside those periods, the page identifies a research queue rather than generating a narrative.

Selection and treatment of statistics

The quantitative layer uses official USGS Data Series 140 XLSX workbooks. The atlas imports every numeric annual cell through 1992 for its nine DS140-backed materials. A separate lazy-loaded library preserves all 92 cataloged commodity workbooks, 122 worksheet series, 956 published measures, and 61,133 numeric observations within the project period.

Every library series retains the commodity, worksheet, source header, year, value, published unit statement, price basis, workbook location, agency URL, access date, extraction status, and missing-cell counts. It does not interpolate, estimate missing cells, or convert withheld and nonnumeric entries to zero.

  • Nominal and real price series remain distinct.
  • Production, imports, exports, consumption, stocks, shipments, and world production remain distinct measures.
  • Separate worksheets, such as bauxite and alumina or titanium sponge and scrap, are never silently combined.
  • The project retains USGS-standardized historical units and discloses that USGS may have converted or estimated earlier source values.
  • Country shares, import dependence, and route volumes remain blank until an official country-level table supports them.

Trade by selected year

The Trade tab keeps four differently scoped layers separate.

  • 1861-1899: the selected year is matched to the published Census Statistical Abstract of the United States: 1948 multi-year average for the broad economic class “crude materials.” This category includes minerals and non-mineral raw materials and is not presented as mineral-specific data.
  • 1900-1992: exact-year national imports and exports are machine-extracted from USGS Data Series 140 commodity workbooks, retaining their standardized physical units and commodity definitions.
  • 1962-1992: UN Comtrade supplies U.S.-reported partner-product context for nine strategic materials plus the separate rare-earth continuity pilot. SITC revisions, ores, compounds, intermediate products, ferroalloys, unwrought metals, alloys, waste, and articles remain separately labeled. Rare-earth mirror reports remain parallel observations.
  • 1989-1992: USITC DataWeb supplies the authoritative U.S.-reported partner-product layer at shared HS6 level, with customs or F.A.S. value and reported quantities.

The UNCCD Knowledge Hub page is retained as a discovery pointer identifying UN Statistics Division as the managing entity; statistical records come from the official UN Comtrade API. No annual value is interpolated from a Census period average, and missing cells are not treated as zero. Partner country does not establish mine origin, ownership, route, end use, import dependence, or strategic importance. FRUS remains the narrative and documentary spine; quantitative layers supply context.

Historical Geostrategic Atlas

Country and territory records store names by period, alternate historical names, present-day names, and selected sovereignty changes. Search may connect a modern name to a historical entity, but the atlas displays the name appropriate to the selected year.

A generated annual evidence ledger covers every year from 1861 through 1992 and every pilot material. It counts checked-in FRUS, statistical, trade, policy, stockpile, and archival-discovery records for the exact selection. Its labels describe evidence availability, not historical importance or completeness.

The atlas uses public-domain Natural Earth 1:110m modern generalized country polygons only for spatial orientation. Natural Earth is not an official U.S. Government evidence source and its geometry is not used to substantiate historical claims. Historical borders are not yet reconstructed. Dated project records control historical names and status.

What atlas symbols mean

FRUS shading measures linked pilot records whose volume span includes the selected year. In Resource Geography, “profile context” means only that a curated country and mineral profile are associated; “year-linked” means at least one checked-in record connects the geography to the selected year. Neither category measures production or supply. Documentary access lines connect countries named in linked negotiations or policy instruments; their width measures linked pilot FRUS records. The Reported Partner Trade lens uses U.S.-reported UN Comtrade SITC values for 1962-1988 and USITC DataWeb HS6 customs values for 1989-1992. These source vintages are never spliced into a continuous index.

Production, supplier-share, infrastructure, alliance-boundary, and strategic-risk map modes remain visibly locked. Each states the fields and official citation needed before activation. Country centroids are never presented as mine, smelter, port, railway, pipeline, base, stockpile-site, or diplomatic-post coordinates.

The vector engine is a pinned, vendored copy of MapLibre GL JS. Every view has a synchronized text panel and accessible table alternative, and shareable URL state records the selected year, material, country, lens, and active layers.

Treaties, agreements, and law

The instruments layer distinguishes formal treaties and executive agreements from purchasing agreements, commodity agreements, concessions, negotiation records, and domestic policy instruments. A FRUS chapter describing negotiations is not upgraded into a formal treaty record without an official title and citation.

Law records identify official title, public-law number, Statutes at Large citation, enactment date, linked entities, and official GovInfo text. Summaries are orientation only; the official text controls.

NARA archival discovery and relevance

The repository includes structured search plans for relevant record groups rather than stored NARA API response content. A deployment proxy holds the API key outside GitHub Pages and requests descriptions on demand. The browser never receives the key.

Returned descriptions may be labeled direct match, probable match, contextual match, or broad archival lead according to visible metadata matching. These labels support triage; they do not establish substantive relevance.

API handling

NARA’s current API terms prohibit publishing the key and state that returned API content should not be cached or stored. The project therefore uses an on-demand proxy with no-store responses rather than a committed static NARA cache.

OCR and manual transcription

No OCR-derived numerical series is treated as authoritative in the current pilot. Future PDF-only ingestion must retain the publication, page or table, original image or stable source URL, OCR method, transcription status, reviewer status, original unit, and any transformation. OCR-assisted values remain “review required” until checked against the page image.

Gaps, uncertainty, and completeness

The interface uses three entity-level states:

Verified pilot
The pilot contains reviewed or directly extracted evidence sufficient for the displayed claim, not comprehensive coverage.
Partial coverage
One or more evidence layers are linked, while named gaps remain.
Research queue
The entity or relationship is structurally represented but requires document-level verification or additional official sources.

These labels describe the project’s coverage, not the historical significance of the subject or the intrinsic reliability of the agency source.

Separating post-1992 Modern Context

Modern Context is outside the historical dataset. It may note current official classifications or why a subject remains relevant, but it does not reorganize the historical record around current policy language or imply that older precedents supply direct prescriptions.

How users should cite the site

Use this portal to locate and connect evidence. Cite the linked FRUS document, statute, treaty publication, official table, or NARA catalog record whenever possible. When citing project-created cross-links or data transformations, include the page title, repository name, version or retrieval date, and the underlying official source.

Suggested project citation:

The United States and Strategic Resources, 1861–1992, Critical Minerals Records Stage v2, public research demonstrator, accessed [date], [page URL].
