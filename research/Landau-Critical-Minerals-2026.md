# Deputy Secretary Landau and the Critical Minerals Imperative: State Department Priorities in 2026

## Executive Summary

In 2026, critical minerals procurement has emerged as the defining operational priority for the U.S. Department of State, with Deputy Secretary Christopher Landau serving as the principal diplomatic driver of that agenda. Landau has personally signed bilateral agreements, witnessed major commercial deals, co-chaired high-level industry task forces, and conducted multi-continent diplomatic travel—all centered on securing supply chains for the rare earth elements, battery metals, and strategic materials that underpin American technological and defense capabilities. This represents a fundamental reorientation of how the State Department deploys its diplomatic workforce: mineral security is no longer the province of specialized trade bureaus alone, but an all-hands priority integrated into virtually every bilateral relationship and overseas mission.

***

## Background: Why Critical Minerals Rose to the Top of the Agenda

The Trump administration's second term inherited a growing strategic vulnerability: China controls nearly 90 percent of global rare earth processing capacity and leads refining for 19 of 20 designated strategic minerals. When Beijing imposed export controls on gallium, germanium, and several rare earth elements in late 2024, in direct response to escalating U.S. tariffs, the resulting disruptions to American manufacturers made the supply-chain vulnerability impossible to ignore.[^1][^2]

A January 2026 Presidential Proclamation formally declared critical mineral imports a national security threat and directed the State Department to pursue bilateral supply agreements rather than impose blanket tariffs—immediately elevating diplomacy as the primary policy instrument. This set the stage for an unprecedented mobilization of State Department resources under Landau's direct supervision.[^3]

Christopher Landau was sworn in as the 23rd Deputy Secretary of State on March 25, 2025. A Harvard College and Harvard Law graduate who clerked for both Justice Scalia and Justice Thomas, Landau previously served as U.S. Ambassador to Mexico (2019–2021), where he presided over the largest U.S. diplomatic mission and brokered entry into force of the USMCA. As Deputy Secretary—effectively the State Department's chief operating officer—he was positioned to translate White House policy directives into operational diplomatic practice across hundreds of overseas posts.[^4]

***

## The February 2026 Critical Minerals Ministerial: Landau's Central Role

The anchor event of the administration's 2026 minerals strategy was the inaugural Critical Minerals Ministerial, hosted by Secretary of State Marco Rubio on February 4, 2026. With representatives from 54 countries and the European Commission in attendance—including 43 foreign ministers—it was described as the largest diplomatic gathering on critical minerals in history.[^1]

Landau's role was not ceremonial. In the two days preceding the ministerial, he was the principal U.S. official overseeing the deal-making and commercial diplomacy that surrounded the summit:

- **The Glencore-Orion MOU (February 3, 2026):** Landau personally witnessed the signing of a non-binding Memorandum of Understanding between Glencore and the U.S.-backed Orion Critical Mineral Consortium for a potential acquisition of a 40 percent stake in Glencore's Democratic Republic of Congo assets—Mutanda Mining and Kamoto Copper Company—at a combined implied enterprise value of approximately $9 billion. This deal, the first major investment following the U.S.-DRC Strategic Partnership Agreement signed in December 2025, was explicitly designed to secure flows of copper and cobalt from the DRC to the United States. Landau stated publicly: "The United States government is fully committed to the Washington Accords and ensuring peace, stability and prosperity throughout the entire Great Lakes region. This proposed transaction reflects the core objectives of the U.S.-DRC Strategic Partnership Agreement."[^5][^1]

- **Industry Task Force Convening (February 4, 2026):** Immediately following the ministerial itself, Landau and Under Secretary Jacob Helberg convened a task force of mining industry leaders to advance priority projects within the new multilateral cooperation framework. This reflected the administration's conviction that minerals diplomacy requires direct private-sector integration, not merely government-to-government agreements.[^6]

- **Ministerial Bilateral Meetings:** On the sidelines of the ministerial, Landau met bilaterally with the foreign ministers of Argentina and Uzbekistan to advance critical minerals cooperation. He also met with Kazakhstan's Foreign Minister Yermek Kosherbayev, where both sides discussed the bilateral agenda including "collaboration in energy and critical minerals". These interactions underscored that Landau was the operational point of contact for turning ministerial commitments into actionable bilateral frameworks.[^7][^8][^9]

At the ministerial, the United States signed 11 new bilateral critical minerals frameworks or MOUs with Argentina, the Cook Islands, Ecuador, Guinea, Morocco, Paraguay, Peru, the Philippines, the UAE, the United Kingdom, and Uzbekistan—adding to 10 similar frameworks signed in the preceding five months and with 17 additional countries whose negotiations had been completed. The State Department's post-ministerial fact sheet cited more than $30 billion in government-mobilized investments, loans, and letters of interest over the preceding six months.[^6]

***

## The Pre-Ministerial Africa Tour: Embedding Minerals in Bilateral Diplomacy

Before the February ministerial, Landau conducted a January 24–February 1 tour of Egypt, Ethiopia, Kenya, and Djibouti alongside General Dagvin Anderson, Commander of U.S. Africa Command. This was significant both symbolically and substantively: it was Landau's first Africa trip as Deputy Secretary, and the co-travel with AFRICOM's commander signaled that critical minerals had been elevated to the nexus of commercial and security diplomacy.[^10]

In Cairo, Landau engaged U.S. companies operating in Egypt and discussed strengthening the U.S.-Egypt economic relationship. In Addis Ababa, he promoted U.S. commercial engagement and met with Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed. In Nairobi—a stop read as particularly significant given Kenya's rare earth potential, including the contested Mrima Hill site—he met Kenyan officials on commercial engagement alongside counterterrorism issues. Djibouti discussions focused on both security cooperation and commercial ties. Throughout the trip, Landau was explicitly tasked with promoting "President Trump's priorities of rebalancing trade, ensuring a positive business environment, and promoting security and peace".[^11][^10]

The timing was deliberate. The Africa tour concluded just two days before the ministerial, with Landau personally ensuring that key African mineral-producing nations—the DRC, Kenya, Guinea, and others—arrived in Washington with momentum already built. The DRC's President Felix Tshisekedi and ministers from Guinea and Kenya all attended the ministerial.[^12]

***

## The Uzbekistan MOU: Landau as Treaty Signatory

On February 4–5, 2026, Landau personally signed the intergovernmental Memorandum of Understanding between the United States and Uzbekistan on "Securing of Supply in the Mining and Processing of Critical Minerals and Rare Earths" alongside Uzbek Foreign Minister Bakhtiyor Saidov. Uzbekistan hosts significant deposits of tungsten, lithium, vanadium, titanium, germanium, and graphite—materials directly on the U.S. critical minerals list.[^9][^13][^14]

The deal was followed on February 18, 2026, by a broader "Joint Investment Framework" between the U.S. International Development Finance Corporation (DFC) and Uzbekistan, proposing a U.S.-Uzbekistan Joint Holding Company for future mineral and infrastructure ventures. Landau's signature on the intergovernmental MOU provided the political authorization that enabled the DFC's investment framework to follow. This two-track approach—Landau signing the diplomatic instrument, DFC deploying the capital—became the model for how State Department employees were expected to understand their role: create the political and legal architecture that enables U.S. commercial investment to flow.[^15]

***

## Chile: Expanding the Hemisphere-Wide Minerals Push

On March 12, 2026, Landau traveled to Santiago, where he signed a Joint Declaration to Establish Consultations on Critical Minerals and Rare Earths with Chilean Foreign Minister Francisco Pérez Mackenna, in a ceremony presided over by newly inaugurated President José Antonio Kast. Chile is the world's leading copper producer and second-largest lithium producer—making it one of the most strategically valuable mineral partners the United States could engage.[^16][^17][^18]

Landau was direct about the stakes: "I believe there is much we can do with the United States and Chile to strengthen the supply chains of these minerals. We will discuss how we can work together." The agreement established a technical consultation mechanism covering four workstreams: supply chain strengthening, joint identification of strategic projects, management of mineral recycling and scrap, and exploration of public and private financing mechanisms. The first round of consultations was scheduled within 15 days of signing.[^17][^16]

The Chile visit was the first of what became a pattern of Landau-led missions to resource-rich nations in the Western Hemisphere. These visits were not standalone diplomatic courtesies but operational engagements tied to specific deliverables—project lists, financing mechanisms, and institutionalized consultation bodies that State Department employees at posts in those countries were expected to advance and monitor.

***

## FORGE: The Institutional Architecture Landau Helped Build

The ministerial produced not only bilateral agreements but a new multilateral architecture: the Forum on Resource Geostrategic Engagement (FORGE), announced by Secretary Rubio as the successor to the Biden-era Minerals Security Partnership. FORGE, initially chaired by South Korea through June 2026, is designed to function as a preferential trade zone for critical minerals, with coordinated price floors to counter Chinese market manipulation.[^19][^20][^21][^22]

Landau's role in FORGE extends beyond ceremonial attendance. The State Department's fact sheet noted his personal involvement in the post-ministerial industry task force that was meant to translate FORGE's framework into actionable project commitments. Analysts at the Atlantic Council described FORGE as "the Trump administration's clearest attempt yet to translate its sizeable ambitions around critical minerals into a functional architecture" and noted that it "reflects a belief that the hardest challenges in the minerals markets are better addressed with partners".[^22][^6]

In May 2026, the administration announced a further multilateral framework: the Quad Critical Minerals Initiative among the United States, Japan, Australia, and India, with commitments to mobilize up to $20 billion in government and private sector support. This expanded the orbit of allied minerals diplomacy that Landau and his counterparts had been building since the February ministerial.

***

## Staffing Up: The Workforce Dimension

A May 29, 2026 Presidential Memorandum authorized federal agencies to hire up to 400 specialists in investment, engineering, finance, and law exclusively to advance critical minerals programs, with pay ceilings of $400,000—explicitly benchmarked to private-sector market rates. The White House's own language described this as necessary to build "a workforce capable of executing major investment programs at the scale and speed required to reduce dependence on foreign sources, strengthen industrial resilience, and protect the Nation's long-term strategic interests".[^23][^24]

This directive has direct implications for State Department personnel. The USGLC's March 2026 issue brief noted that "the State Department and America's economic development agencies are already driving initiatives from Africa to Europe to the Indo-Pacific to increase U.S. access to critical minerals, strengthen supply chains, and deploy 'wrap-around' investments in communities". Under Secretary Jacob Helberg's economic bureau (E bureau) and Assistant Secretary Caleb Orr's Bureau of Economic and Business Affairs have been the internal State Department leads for the minerals agenda—but Landau's office has provided the senior diplomatic imprimatur that gives those efforts weight in bilateral engagements.[^25][^26][^27]

At the SelectUSA Investment Summit in May 2026, Landau explicitly framed minerals as inseparable from the investment promotion mission he was delivering to the business community: the Trump administration had "committed to strengthening domestic capacity in sectors like advanced technology, critical minerals, energy, and manufacturing," and State Department missions worldwide were instructed to connect interested foreign and domestic investors with U.S. minerals opportunities.[^28]

## Implications for State Department Employees

Landau's engagement with critical minerals is not simply the activity of one senior official—it reflects a directive to reorient the diplomatic workforce across all bureaus and overseas posts. Several operational implications for State Department personnel are evident from the 2026 activity:

**1. Minerals as a cross-cutting bureau priority.** The Bureau of Economic and Business Affairs (EB) and the new economic statecraft framework under Under Secretary Helberg have been designated as the lead internal coordinators, but the Africa Bureau, the Western Hemisphere Bureau, the Bureau of South and Central Asian Affairs, and the Near East Bureau all have active minerals mandates given the geographic distribution of critical mineral deposits.[^25]

**2. Embassies as deal-sourcing and deal-enabling platforms.** The SelectUSA remarks and the Chile/Uzbekistan missions both illustrate that Landau expects overseas missions to actively connect U.S. investors with host-country opportunities, perform due diligence facilitation, and flag political and regulatory impediments to projects. The USGLC brief explicitly noted that State Department engagement—"wrap-around investments in communities" including rule of law, anti-corruption, and workforce programs—is considered essential to making mineral projects commercially viable.[^28][^25]

**3. Security-minerals nexus at posts.** The Africa tour with AFRICOM's commander and the April 2026 Chile minerals-and-security agreement signed by Under Secretary for Arms Control Thomas DiNanno both signal that minerals diplomacy extends into the national security and defense communities. Officers at posts in mineral-rich nations are expected to integrate minerals intelligence and deal facilitation into security-focused reporting and engagement.[^30]

**4. New institutional mechanisms require staff engagement.** FORGE, the FORGE action plan with Mexico, and the Quad Critical Minerals Initiative Framework all create ongoing mechanisms requiring State Department participation at technical and working levels. Personnel assigned to multilateral coordination and to bilateral relationships with FORGE member states will find minerals coordination among their primary workload.[^20][^19]

**5. Legislative context.** Congress has engaged bipartisan support for this agenda, with the DOMINANCE Act (introduced by Reps. Young Kim and Ami Bera) seeking to "formalize State Department leadership on energy diplomacy," and the Critical Minerals Partnership Act (Senators Shaheen and Curtis) codifying U.S. participation in the Minerals Security Partnership/FORGE framework. These legislative initiatives would further institutionalize the State Department's minerals role.[^25]

***

## Assessment and Strategic Context

The Chatham House analysis of the February ministerial noted that "the U.S. is the first nation to rightly make minerals supply a national foreign policy priority and understand that disrupting the status quo requires a significant amount of cash". That assessment captures the dual nature of Landau's role: he is simultaneously a diplomatic operator (signing MOUs, building coalitions) and a commercial facilitator (connecting U.S. capital with resource-rich governments).[^31]

The critical tension identified by multiple analysts is between the speed of the political commitments and the pace of actual mine development. As the Atlantic Council observed, the ministerial "delivered speed and ambition" but "execution and durability are the harder tests ahead". For State Department employees, this means that the agreements Landau has signed and the frameworks he has launched require sustained diplomatic investment—not one-time signings but ongoing bilateral engagement, project facilitation, and monitoring of whether partner governments follow through on their commitments.[^1]

Chatham House also pointed to the importance of building bureaucratic "vested interests" around implementation—creating departments and agencies whose institutional missions are tied to the success of the minerals agenda, making it harder for any future administration to reverse course. The May 2026 workforce memo authorizing up to 400 new hires at $400,000 salaries is precisely that kind of institutional entrenchment. For the State Department's existing workforce, it signals that minerals expertise is now a career asset and a performance expectation across a wider range of positions than ever before.[^31]

***

## Conclusion

Deputy Secretary Christopher Landau has made critical minerals procurement a defining feature of his tenure, functioning as the State Department's lead commercial diplomat on the issue. Through a combination of multi-continent diplomatic travel, direct deal witnessing and MOU signing, industry task force leadership, and bilateral ministerial engagement, he has embedded critical minerals acquisition into the daily operational reality of U.S. diplomacy in 2026. For State Department employees across geographic and functional bureaus, this translates into a clear directive: every bilateral relationship with a mineral-rich nation, every overseas commercial engagement, and every multilateral forum participation carries the expectation that minerals supply chain resilience will be advanced. The institutional architecture—FORGE, Project Vault, the DFC's Orion CMC, the Quad Critical Minerals Framework—has been built; staffing it with capable personnel and executing on the agreements already signed is now the primary challenge.
